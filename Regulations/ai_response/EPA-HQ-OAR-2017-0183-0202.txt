**Scientists Raise Concerns on Mercury Pollution's Impact on Public Health in Southern Florida**

In a letter addressed to the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency, researchers from Florida Gulf Coast University have outlined alarming findings regarding mercury levels in Southern Florida's environment, especially highlighting the implications on public health. The letter was signed by Thomas M. Missimer, a professor at the U.A. Whitaker College of Engineering, and his colleagues James H. MacDonald Jr., Seneshaw Tsegaye, Serge Thomas, Christopher M. Teaf, Douglas Covert, and Zoie R. Kassis.

According to Missimer, “Mercury is a toxic metal that can accumulate in the environment and has serious health impacts based on exposure.” The team's findings indicate that Southern Florida has one of the highest mercury deposition rates in the United States, with significant release occurring primarily due to both natural and anthropogenic sources.

MacDonald Jr. emphasized the historical context of mercury in the region, stating, "Southern Florida contains a very large natural reservoir of legacy mercury in the peatlands of the Everglades." This mercury, deposited over thousands of years, has been exacerbated by modern activities such as agriculture and residential development that contribute to pollution.

The article highlights the health risks of mercury exposure, particularly through consumption of contaminated fish. Teaf stated, “High levels of mercury have been recorded in various fish species, prompting health alerts about consumption rates.” These risks impact local communities, with recommended fish consumption limits often being exceeded in caught species.

Kassis further pointed out that existing regulations and assessments may not fully account for the unique environmental conditions found in the region, stating, “Significant gaps in research on the impacts of atmospheric mercury deposition need urgent attention.” She urged for systematic studies to assess both ecological and health impacts.

The letter concluded with a call to action for comprehensive research efforts to evaluate the dangers posed by mercury in Southern Florida, as well as a thorough reassessment of current regulations. The findings were formally presented to the environmental agency on January 22, 2024, addressing the need for heightened awareness about mercury pollution and public wellbeing.

5.4. Atmospheric Emissions of Hg from Cement Plants
In China, the cement industry has become one of the largest emission sources for Hg
[169,170], and is a source of mercury discharge in the United States [171–173]. The source
of the Hg is not primarily from the limestone treated in the kiln, but from the fuel used to
fire the kiln. There are two cement production facilities located in southern Florida run by
Cemex and Titan America. The estimated annual emissions of Hg for these two facilities
are 54.9 and 95.7 kg/yr, respectively [172]. Therefore, the atmospheric flux of Hg from
cement plants into the southern Florida atmosphere is approximately 150.6 kg/yr.

8. Conclusions
Southern Florida has the highest Hg concentration in the atmosphere within the
continentalUnitedStates,withanaverageannualwetdepositionrate(2021)of16.0µg/m2.
Thetotaldepositionofallformsofnewmercuryisestimatedtobe411.2kg/yforthearea
includingMonroe,Dade,Broward,PalmBeach,Hendry,Collier,andLeecounties,which
is an area of 25,720 km2. The highest influx of naturally occurring Hg is from Saharan
dust, marine evasion, and global aerosols, which in combination contribute between
257.1 and 514.2 kg/y. Another major contributor of Hg to the atmosphere of southern
Florida is the remobilization of legacy Hg from the Everglades. Prior to the impacts of
